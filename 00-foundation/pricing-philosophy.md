# Pricing Philosophy

## The Core Belief

**Price is a positioning decision, not a math problem.**

Hormozi's wine experiment is the foundational data point: identical product, higher price → higher perceived value, even when the buyer can't tell the difference blind. Price *signals* value. Underpricing signals "this might not work."

## Rules

### 1. Never compete on price.
The cheapest digital product in any category is a magnet for the worst customers — high refund rates, high support load, low LTV, low referrals. Race-to-the-bottom pricing is a death spiral. We refuse to play that game.

### 2. Price for value delivered, not effort spent.
A 10-page PDF that saves a buyer 40 hours is worth $97, not $9. The buyer doesn't pay for our effort — they pay for their outcome.

### 3. The 10x value-to-price ratio is the floor.
At every rung, the perceived value should be 10x the price. A $49 PDF should feel like it's worth $500 to the right buyer. If the math doesn't work, either raise the perceived value (bonuses, framing) or lower the price — never ship at a 2x ratio.

### 4. Trust tripwire = $49 by default.
$49 is the impulse-purchase ceiling for a knowledge product. Above $50, buyers move from "yes" to "let me think about it." Every first-rung product targets $49 unless there's a compelling reason to go lower (lead magnet at $9–$19) or higher (a $97 product that *only* works at that price point).

### 5. Round to the right anchor.
$47, $49, $97, $147, $297, $497, $997, $1,997, $2,997, $4,997. Avoid $50, $100, $200 — they read as round-number defaults. Avoid $19.99 — it reads as discount-bin pricing for an info product.

### 6. Always have a higher-priced offer visible.
When buyers see Rung 5 at $997 on the same page (or even just referenced), Rung 1 at $49 looks like a steal. The decoy is the strategy. Never sell a single product in isolation.

### 7. Founding-price windows, not permanent discounts.
Launch at a 30–50% founding-member discount for 48–72 hours, then go to full price. Permanent discount codes destroy the perception of value. One-time founding pricing rewards your most loyal buyers without poisoning the price.

### 8. Bundle to lift AOV, never to discount the bundle below the cheapest unit.
Three PDFs at $49 each = $147. Bundle at $97 (33% off). Don't bundle at $49 — that destroys the standalone price of every PDF in the bundle.

### 9. Risk reversal pays for itself.
Every product has a money-back guarantee (30-day default, conditional on completing the work for higher rungs). Empirical refund rates: 2–5%. Conversion lift from including the guarantee: 3–5x. The math is not close.

### 10. Raise prices, never lower.
After 100 sales at $49, raise to $59. After 250 at $59, raise to $79. The product gets better with each version (testimonials, case studies, refinement); the price reflects that. Lowering price is a panic move and signals weakness.

## Pricing Decision Tree

```
Is this a lead magnet?
├── Yes → Free or $9 max
└── No
    │
    Is the buyer a first-time buyer in our funnel?
    ├── Yes → $49 (trust tripwire)
    └── No
        │
        Is this a bundle / system / template pack?
        ├── Yes → $97–$497
        └── No
            │
            Does this involve our time?
            ├── Yes → $997+
            └── No → $97–$297 (premium info product)
```

## What We Will Not Do

- "Pay what you want" pricing on flagship products (signals zero confidence).
- Permanent 50% off "sales" (poisons the price anchor permanently).
- Bundling unrelated products to inflate perceived value (destroys trust).
- Charging less than the cost of one hour of our time for any product that requires our time.
