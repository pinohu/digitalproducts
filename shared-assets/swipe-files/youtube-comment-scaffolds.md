# YouTube Comment Scaffolds — Value-First Distribution

> *Reusable across products. First deployed for "The Good Parts of SuiteDash" (Mike Lambert's channel). Same scaffolds work for any product where YouTube tutorial channels host the buyer audience mid-learning-curve.*

---

## The Rules

1. **Always answer the actual question first.** Generic comments are invisible.
2. **Never link in the comment.** Profile bio is the funnel surface. Linking in comments is the #1 way to get flagged as spam and get the comment deleted.
3. **Vary every comment.** YouTube's spam detection flags repeated phrasing across a channel. Change at least 30% of the wording each time.
4. **Engage on replies.** A comment with 3 back-and-forth replies stays at the top of the comment list. The conversation is what makes other readers click your profile.
5. **Two comments per video maximum.** One initial substantive answer, one optional follow-up if a real exchange develops. More than that looks like astroturfing.

## Scaffold A — "Confused about which feature to use"

> The thing that helped me when I was stuck on this: [feature X] and [feature Y] do almost the same thing on the surface, but [feature X] is for [specific use case] and [feature Y] is for [specific use case]. If your client only needs [outcome], skip [feature Y] entirely — it adds two settings menus you'll never touch. I learned this the slow way running it across multiple businesses.

**When to use:** Commenter is asking about feature differentiation or expressing confusion between similar features.

**What's working here:** Specific use cases anchor your authority. The "I learned this the slow way" line signals real experience without being preachy.

---

## Scaffold B — "Is X worth setting up?"

> Honest answer: it depends on whether you're billing recurring or one-off. For one-off project work, [feature] is overkill — you'll spend more time configuring it than you'll save. For recurring, it pays back in week two. I'd hold off until you've onboarded your third client and can see the pattern.

**When to use:** Commenter is in evaluation mode about a specific feature.

**What's working here:** The if/then structure forces specificity. "I'd hold off until your third client" gives a concrete decision rule, not vague advice.

---

## Scaffold C — "I'm overwhelmed by everything in here"

> You're not alone — this is the #1 reason people churn off [tool] even though it's the best in its category. The fix isn't "learn it all." The fix is to ignore 80% of it on purpose. Pick three features ([feature A], [feature B], [feature C]), get those working with one real client, then add one feature per week. Trying to set everything up before going live is the trap.

**When to use:** Commenter expresses overwhelm, confusion, or the "where do I start" question.

**What's working here:** Names the trap by name. "Ignore 80% of it on purpose" is a counterintuitive frame that sticks. The three-feature recommendation is concrete enough to act on tonight.

---

## Scaffold D — "[Tool] vs [Competitor]"

> I've used both. [Competitor] wins if [specific narrow use case]. [Tool] wins if you need [specific thing] AND [specific thing] in the same product — which is most service businesses. The catch is the learning curve is real and the help docs assume you already know the product. Budget a weekend, not an evening.

**When to use:** Commenter is comparing tools.

**What's working here:** You acknowledge the competitor's strengths, which builds trust. "Budget a weekend, not an evening" is a small, helpful, specific honesty that signals you've actually used it.

---

## Scaffold E — "Specific setup question"

> The trick with [feature] that's not obvious from the docs: [specific tactical insight in 2–3 sentences]. Once you see it that way, the rest of the menu makes sense. Took me about 4 hours of trial and error to figure out — would've saved that time if anyone had just said it plainly.

**When to use:** Commenter has a specific tactical setup question.

**What's working here:** The "took me 4 hours of trial and error" framing positions you as someone who has done the work. It also subtly suggests there's value in *not* having to learn it that way.

## Channel Rotation Strategy

**Per-video frequency:**
- Don't comment on every video. Pick 20 videos in the channel — the ones with the most active comment sections.
- Spread the 20 comments over 14 days (~1.5 per day average).
- Mix scaffolds — don't run all-A then all-B.

**Profile setup before commenting:**
- Channel banner: clean, professional, on-brand
- Channel description: one-line positioning + link to ikeohu.com OR Gumroad pre-sell page
- About section: short bio that signals expertise without selling

**Tracking spreadsheet columns:**
- Video URL
- Comment timestamp
- Scaffold used (A/B/C/D/E)
- Replies received (yes/no, count)
- Profile visits (visible in YouTube Studio after the comment is up)

## Example: Filling Scaffold A for SuiteDash (Mike Lambert's channel)

Generic version:
> [feature X] and [feature Y] do almost the same thing on the surface, but [feature X] is for [specific use case]...

SuiteDash-specific:
> Auto-Templates and FLOWs do almost the same thing on the surface, but Auto-Templates are for "fire this sequence when X happens" (event-driven) and FLOWs are for "walk a user through these steps in order" (intake / onboarding). If your client only needs to be welcomed and onboarded, skip Auto-Templates entirely — start with one FLOW. I learned this after building three identical workflows in both modules.

That's a real, useful answer. It positions you as someone with multi-business experience without being a sales pitch.

## What NOT to Comment

- Never: "Great video! Check out my [thing]"
- Never: "I have a free guide on this — link in bio"
- Never: pure agreement ("So true!" "Yes!")
- Never: anything that contradicts the video creator's authority unprompted (this is their channel)

The line: be the helpful expert in someone else's classroom, not the substitute teacher.

---

## When This Stops Working

If your YouTube profile starts getting ghosted (comments held for review, low view counts on profile clicks despite high comment engagement), the algorithm has probably down-ranked you for promotional behavior.

Recovery: stop commenting on the channel for 7 days. When you return, lead with 3 comments that have NO link in profile bio at all (just text positioning). Re-introduce the link slowly.

Better still: don't trip the wire. Two comments per video maximum, profile link to a non-Gumroad domain (your authority site), no link drops in comments themselves.
